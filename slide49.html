The Eschatological Vision

• Ultimate Fulfillment: "History is moving toward its fulfillment in Christ's return and the new creation"
• Already/Not Yet: The kingdom is already present but not yet fully realized
• Continuity: The good of this world will be purified and transformed, not destroyed
• Present Responsibility: Eschatological hope motivates present action for justice
• Universal Scope: God's plan encompasses all creation and all people
"We do not know the time for the consummation of the earth and of humanity, nor do we know how all things will be transformed. As deformed by sin, the shape of this world will pass away; but we are taught that God is preparing a new dwelling place and a new earth where justice will abide, and whose blessedness will answer and surpass all the longings for peace which spring up in the human heart."

Eschatological Tensions in Christian Life

Tension Distortion to Avoid Balanced Approach
Already/Not Yet Either over-realized eschatology (kingdom fully present) or under-realized eschatology (kingdom only future) Recognizing signs of the kingdom now while working and waiting for its fullness
This World/Next World Either worldly activism without transcendent hope or otherworldly escapism Transforming this world in light of the world to come
Individual/Cosmic Either individualistic salvation or impersonal cosmic process Personal salvation within the context of cosmic redemption
Continuity/Discontinuity Either complete rupture with present or mere extension of present Transformation that both preserves and transcends
Divine Action/Human Effort Either passive waiting or presumptuous human control Active cooperation with God's grace
Pastoral Application:
The eschatological vision of Gaudium et Spes provides a framework for addressing contemporary issues like environmental care. Rather than seeing the earth as disposable because of Christ's return, the document's emphasis on continuity between this world and the new creation motivates Christians to be good stewards of creation. This has been further developed in Pope Francis's encyclical Laudato Si', which connects care for our common home with the ultimate destiny of creation.

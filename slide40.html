Peace and War

• Peace as Goal: "Peace is not merely the absence of war, but the establishment of a just order"
• Causes of War: Injustice, inequality, and lack of respect for human rights
• Nuclear Concern: Special concern about weapons of mass destruction
• Just War Tradition: Strict conditions for legitimate defense
• Peacebuilding: Active work for justice, development, and human rights
"Peace is not merely the absence of war; nor can it be reduced solely to the maintenance of a balance of power between enemies; nor is it brought about by dictatorship. Instead, it is rightly and appropriately called an enterprise of justice. Peace results from that order structured into human society by its divine Founder, and actualized by men as they thirst after ever greater justice."

Just War Criteria

Category Criteria Explanation
Jus ad Bellum
(Right to War)		 Just Cause Defense against unjust aggression
Legitimate Authority Decision by proper public authority
Last Resort All peaceful alternatives exhausted
Probability of Success Reasonable hope of achieving just peace
Jus in Bello
(Conduct in War)		 Discrimination Protection of non-combatants
Proportionality Force used proportionate to good achieved
Right Intention Aim for just peace, not revenge or conquest
Contemporary Development:
Since Gaudium et Spes, Catholic teaching on war and peace has evolved significantly. Pope John Paul II and subsequent popes have emphasized the difficulty of meeting just war criteria in modern warfare, especially with weapons of mass destruction. Pope Francis has further developed this teaching, stating in Fratelli Tutti that "it is very difficult nowadays to invoke the rational criteria elaborated in earlier centuries to speak of the possibility of a 'just war'." This represents a development of the principles articulated in Gaudium et Spes.
